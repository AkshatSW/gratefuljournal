Connecting acknowledging & accepting your emotions

Impact of keeping emotions bottled up

The Majority of people hold in their emotions rather than processing them or expressing them because they think the alternative is someone who cries all the time, is incredibly angry and erratic, and can't control themselves. But suppressing emotions is not a good long-term strategy It has marked negative effects on your emotional and physical health.

Effects on emotional wellbeing

If we push our feelings down and down and try to avoid them, eventually, they will explode out more fiercely than before. It’s like a pressure cooker, if you keep the steam within, it will explode at some point.
However, when we process our emotions regularly rather than ignoring them, we can lead a much happier and more fulfilled life without the sudden emotional outbursts, just as in a boiling pot, if the water evaporates continuously there is no pent-up steam generated and thus no explosion.
“Because men are taught more to display less emotion, they, in particular, run the risk of exploding in rages as they finally unleash their pent-up emotions, and suffering long-term physical and psychological damage for failing to manage stress healthily.

Effects on physical wellbeing

1. Your Stomach Twists Itself Into Knots: The chronic stress that comes from unresolved emotions can trigger your sympathetic nervous This slows digestion, resulting in gas, bloating, constipation, vomiting, and, occasionally, ulcers.

2. Your Neck And Shoulders Scream From Stress: The corrugator muscles in the forehead and brow tighten in response to emotional stress, producing a frown, and a tight corrugator muscle is often a good indicator of stress throughout the entire body, psychologist Daniel Goleman

Stress Might Mess With Your Heart : One study conducted by psychologists from the Harvard School of Public Health and the University of Rochester found that suppressing emotions may increase the risk of dying from heart disease and certain forms of cancer. When more complicated feelings of sadness and shame are buried take the form of anger causing a rush of stress hormones which causes blood vessels to tighten as blood pressure increases, which can wear on artery walls over time.

5. Lowers immunity: Studies by Pennebaker and his colleagues (1997) demonstrated that individuals who repress their emotions also suppress their body’s immunity, making them more vulnerable to a variety of illnesses ranging from common colds to cancer

Daily activities to process emotions

1. Journal: Write about how you are feeling, and any thoughts or emotions that are lingering in your mind. It’s helpful to acknowledge both pleasant and unpleasant emotions. You can choose from a variety of formats based on what works best for you. You can find some journaling templates in this worksheet on page

a. Freestyle journaling: Write whatever comes to your mind in any way you like. Just keep writing without judging yourself and without any filters. Honesty is key.
b. Structured: You can follow any of the templates shared in the workbook below.

Journal Formats

I. Identifying Emtoions

a. Today I am happy about...
b. Sad/worries about
c. Angry/frustrated about
d. Excited about

II. Gratitude journal:

a. What went well today
b. Who helped or supported me
c. What am I grateful for

III. 10 minutes to reflect on your day:

Reflect on following things and journal it everyday
a. 3 Moments You'd like to remember.
b. One of the day's triumphs, big or small
c. One of the day's challenges, big or small.
d. One thing you learned from your triumph or challenge
e. One idea from today that you'd like to explore further

IV. Processing the day


a. What did I accomplish today
b. What all I put in my best effort in
c. What did I learn today?
d. How do I feel?

3. Emotion log: Keep a chart paper where you log in how you feel. You can use emoticons or can use words to express yourself. This method helps in gently being mindful of our emotions and acknowledging them. It also helps us objectively see how we have been feeling for over a week or month and helps us recognize when we may need to pay attention to or process some emotion a little more deeply

Ways to practice Write the emotion you are experiencing each day before you start work and/or at the end of the day

4. Connect & accept your thoughts and emotions with love: Checking in with your body is a great way of understanding and acknowledging your emotions. Follow the following steps:

  1. a. Sit in a comfortable posture
  2. b. Bring awareness to the breath moving in and out at the tip of your nose
  3. c. Allow yourself to become aware of your thoughts one by one, and just go a little deeper. For each thought:
  4. i. Become aware of the thought and emotions
  5. ii. The thoughts attached to this thought and emotions. Label them
  6. iii. Emotions attached to this thought and emotions. Label them
  7. iv. Sensations in your body attached to this thought and emotions. Label them
  8. v. Then say thank you to the thoughts and emotions and allow them to pass out of your head
  9. d. Repeat the above till no more thoughts and emotions are there within. Then stay connected to quietness.
  10. e. When comfortable – open your eyes.

5. Expressing love to self: Hoʻoponopono is a great activity to connect with self and develop a healthy relationship with self. Say the following to yourself:

  1. a. I Am sorry for …….
  2. b. I thank you for….
  3. c. I Love you for….
  4. d. I accept you for who you are
