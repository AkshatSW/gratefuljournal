1. Wim Hof breathing :

Step 1: Settle down & connect with your body: Get Comfortable. Sit in a meditation posture, close your eyes connect with each part of your body, and slowly bring your attention to your breath take 10 slower deeper, and longer breaths.

Step 2: 30 fast breaths:Then Take 30 Deep Fast Breaths. 1 sec inhale 1 sec exhale from your nose and try to clear your mind from any kind of worry

Step 3: The Hold (1.00 - 1.30 min): After the 30th exhalation, inhale one final time, as deeply as you can and exhale completely and hold for 1 min in the first round and 1.30 min in the next 2 rounds

Step 4: Recovery Breath: After the hold, take a deep inhalation and hold the breath inside for 15 seconds as a recovery breath and exhale.

PLEASE NOTE: Repeat the same cycle 3 times back to back

Last Step: Come back to the normal rhythm of breathing, take 10 deep breaths again, be aware of your body, and your breath, come back into the here and now, move your body slowly and open your eyes.

4. Mindful eating :

Mindful eating (i.e., paying attention to our food, on purpose, moment by moment, without judgment) is an approach to food that focuses on individuals’ sensual awareness of the food and their experience of the food. It has little to do with calories, carbohydrates, fat, or protein. The purpose of mindful eating is not to lose weight, although it is highly likely that those who adopt this style of eating will lose weight. The intention is to help individuals savour the moment and the food and encourage their full presence for the eating experience.

How to do mindful eating

  1. Come to the table with an appetite — but not when ravenously hungry.
  2. Appreciate & be grateful for your food, and say thank you
  3. Start with a small portion.
  4. Bring all your senses to the meal.
  5. Take small bites.
  6. Chew thoroughly, be conscious of every bite
  7. Eat slowly & continue to appreciate the food.
Finish the meal & thank the food again.

5. Body Scan :

Body scan meditation is a good way to release physical tension you might not even realize you're experiencing. Body scanning involves paying attention to parts of the body and bodily sensations in a gradual sequence from feet to head. By mentally scanning yourself, you bring awareness to every single part of your body, noticing any aches, pains, tension, or general discomfort. The goal is not to relieve the pain completely, but to get to know and learn from it so you can better manage it.

6. 4-7-8 breathing :

The 4-7-8 breathing technique, also known as “relaxing breath,” involves breathing in for 4 seconds, holding the breath for 7 seconds, and exhaling for 8 seconds.

  1. Step 1. To practice 4-7-8 breathing, find a place to sit or lie down comfortably. Be sure you practice good posture, especially when starting out. If you’re using the technique to fall asleep, lying down is best.
  2. Step 2. First, let your lips part. Make a whooshing sound, exhaling completely through your mouth.
  3. Step 3. Next Close your lips and inhale silently through your nose as you count to 4 in your head ( visualise an energy flow from the naval to the crown while inhaling )
  4. Step 4. Hold your breath in the crown for a count of 7
  5. Step 5. Part your lips slightly and exhale completely through your mouth making a whoosh sound for a count of 8

  1. Practice this pattern for 4 - 8 full breaths and can gradually increase
  2. Please Note: This breathing technique shouldn’t be practiced in a setting where you’re not prepared to fully relax. While it doesn’t necessarily have to be used for falling asleep, it can still put the practitioner into a state of deep relaxation. Make sure you don’t need to be fully alert immediately after practicing your breathing cycles.

7. Gratitude meditation :

If you want a dynamic and effective way to quickly improve your life, and bring happiness, harmony and success to it, look no further than the practice of gratitude.
Gratitude is a deeply undervalued trait in today’s fast-paced, technology-infused world. Many of us have little time to spare. Any free time we are able to enjoy, we usually spend in an attempt to catch our breath before plunging back into the chaos.
The practice of gratitude can have a deep and lasting impact on our well-being. The small investment of time it requires is well worth the effort, and with regular practice, gratitude can become a daily habit that permeates your life.
Gratitude refers to the acknowledgement and appreciation for the things, people, and circumstances of your life. Grateful Mantra Meditation

Part 1: Grateful mantra meditation :
Too often we find ourselves caught up in the difficulties of our lives. We get bogged down with the many challenges we face, and sometimes find it easier to focus on the negative than on the positive.
This is perfectly natural. But if you find yourself wanting to steer your thoughts in a more positive direction, then a gratitude meditation may do the trick.
We take a lot of things for granted without realizing it. When we focus on the things that go wrong, we often overlook the great multitude of things that are going right in our lives.
Gratitude meditations will help you remember the small blessings you may take for granted in your day-to-day life. First, find a comfortable position to rest in, either in a chair, or on a comfortable flat surface.

  1. 1. Take a moment to center yourself. Turn off the ringer on your phone to ensure you won’t be disturbed. If you’re able, close the door to the room you’re in.
  2. 2. Now, take a deep breath through your nose. Fill your lungs entirely with air. Let your breath out through your mouth.
  3. 3. Take a few more deep breaths this way. In through the nose, filling the lungs with air, and out through the mouth.
  4. 4. Now, we’ll practice a gratitude mantra.The mantra is: I am grateful for ______________.
  5. 5. Sounds pretty simple, right? All you need to do is fill in the blank with something you’re grateful for
  6. 6. We will repeat this mantra 10 times. Each time you repeat the mantra, try to come up with something different that you’re thankful for. If you’re having trouble coming up with things, start small, focusing on your immediate environment
  7. 7. You could be thankful for the breeze coming through the window, cooling your face. Or you could be grateful for the soft, plush surface you’re resting on.
    Here are some suggestions to help you get started:
  8. I am grateful for the air in my lungs.
  9. I am grateful for my heart that beats and keeps me alive.
  10. I am grateful for my senses that help me perceive the world.
  11. I am grateful for my comfortable bed.
  12. I am grateful for my clothes that protect me and keep me warm.
  13. I am grateful for the tree in my yard that is beautiful to look at.
  14. I am grateful for the sun that shines through my window and brightens the room.
  15. I am grateful for the food in my fridge.
  16. I am grateful for my job that enables me to provide for myself and/or my loved ones.
  17. I am grateful for my family that cares for me.
  18. I am grateful for my pets that cheer me up.

Part 2: The Yin Yang Gratitude Practice ( Advance )

It’s not easy to focus on the positive when you feel overwhelmed by how difficult your present circumstances may be. This meditation will help you understand that in any challenge, there also exists a lesson. Without darkness, we would not be able to perceive the light.

  1. 1. Seat yourself in a comfortable position and take a few grounding breaths.
  2. 2. Draw your attention to something that’s currently troubling you. It’s best to start small with this practice. Draw on a minor annoyance or irritation that’s been bothering you. For example, perhaps the room you’re in is cold and drafty.
  3. 3. Identify something unpleasant you’re currently faced with, and then see if you can flip the negative circumstance to find a positive aspect. For example, perhaps the room you’re in is cold and drafty, but it has a large, beautiful window that lets in plenty of light.
  4. 4. As you continue to identify things you’re unhappy with in your life, do your best to find a positive opportunity that the circumstance provides. Perhaps you have a long commute to work that you find stressful. A positive spin on this situation could be that your commute gives you the opportunity to enjoy some great music, listen to a cool podcast, or even practice some mindfulness on the way to work.
  5. 5. Seeking the positive within the negative won’t be easy at first, but this is a skill that will spill over into many other areas of your life.

8. Trataka:

Trataka is a Sanskrit word, which means "to look" or "to gaze." As such, this meditation technique involves starting at a single point of focus.

How to practice

  1. 1.Sit in a meditation pose in front of a candle. Place the candle about an arm’s length away from you with the wick of the candle at the same height as your chest. If the candle is placed too high, it can create tension at the eyebrow centre, or produce a burning sensation in the eyes. The flame should be still and not moving in a draft. Close your eyes. Mentally repeat your Mantra as in meditation
  2. 2. Open the eyes and look at the flame without blinking. The flame has three zones of colour. At the base of the wick is a reddish colour, in the middle it is bright white and at the tip it is slightly smoky. Concentrate on the upper part of the flame where it is brightest.
  3. 3. Close the eyes again. If the image of the flame appears within, gently concentrate on that image without creating any tension. Try not to pursue or hold onto the image, otherwise, it will fade and disappear.
  4. 4. Repeat the practice 3 times.
  5. 5. The practice time should gradually build. In the initial stages, look at the flame only for about 10-15 seconds. Slowly increase this time, so that after about one year you can look at the flame for 1 minute and then concentrate on the inner image with closed eyes for about 4 minutes. Under no circumstances should this recommended length of time be exceeded.
  6. 6. One may also practice Trataka while looking at a white point on black paper, or at a black point on white paper. When one concentrates on a white point, one sees this as a black image when the eyes are closed and vice versa with a black point.

10. Five-finger breathing :

Five-finger breathing reboots the computer,” Dr. Brewer said. “It takes so many sense modalities to do it, It calms you down immediately activating your parasympathetic nervous system and releasing happy chemicals

  1. 1. Start by placing one hand in front of you or on the ground.
  2. 2. Spread your fingers apart.
  3. 3. Using the pointer finger of the opposite hand, begin to trace up the outside of the thumb as you inhale deeply.
  4. 4. Trace down the inside of the thumb as you exhale.
  5. 5. Repeat this for the remaining fingers making sure to inhale as you trace up the finger and exhale as you trace down.
  6. 6. Pause afterward to reflect on feelings and repeat as needed.
